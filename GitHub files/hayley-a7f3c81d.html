Key Terms

Quick reference for the language used on your dashboard.
CM (Contribution Margin)
Revenue − COGS − Contribution Expenses (Sales/Account team salaries + super + commissions). The cash left after the team that produced the revenue is paid. Your CM bonus is calculated incrementally above the $1M baseline.
LTM (Last Twelve Months)
Rolling sum of the last 4 complete quarters from Xero. Drives your projected commission and the maintenance bonus calculation.
YoY (Year-over-Year)
Same metric this quarter vs the same quarter last year. Shows whether you're growing the business compared to the previous period.
Maintenance Bonus
If the company stays profitable at $1M+ CM with no growth, you still earn an $11,200 baseline (inc super). This protects you from being penalised for steady-state years.
CM Baseline ($1M)
The floor below which CM bonus = $0. Above $1M, the Maintenance Milestone bonus begins ramping; above $1.1M, the growth bands kick in.
Maintenance Milestone
Flat $11,200 bonus (inc super) that ramps linearly from $1.0M to $1.1M CM. Stated super-inclusive so you see the full benefit value — equivalent to $10,000 ex super + 12% super added on top, but presented as one figure matching how the rest of your plan is framed.
Super-Inclusive
The dollar figures already include the 12% superannuation. So a $46k commission means $41k paid into your bank (pre-tax) and $5k paid into your super fund — $46k total in your name.
75% Quarterly / 25% Holdback
Each quarter pays 75% of commission earned to that point. The remaining 25% is held back and reconciled at year-end based on actual full-year CM.
Quarterly True-Up
Each quarter we look at cumulative CM, calculate the commission earned to date, subtract what's been paid in earlier quarters, and pay the difference (capped at 75% per quarter).
Total Package
Base salary + super on base + CM commission. The full cost-to-MEC of employing you for the year.
KPI Multiplier
A multiplier applied to your variable comp once KPIs go live. Indicative range is roughly 0.7× to 1.3×, but the exact range will be confirmed before activation. Currently locked at 1.0× — KPIs are still in DRAFT.

Why your plan looks like this

Your role is to make the whole business work — not to personally generate sales — so your variable compensation is tied to business-level performance rather than personal GP. Specifically, your plan is anchored entirely on Contribution Margin (CM).

In April 2026 we agreed your base salary moves to $120k, up from your previous package. The agreement was that the higher base needs to be paid for by the business performing well enough to absorb it. The plan trigger is:

  • Maintain Contribution Margin — variable comp triggers when annual CM passes $1.0M (the level of business performance that supports the higher base). A flat $11,200 Maintenance Milestone (inc super) ramps in linearly between $1.0M and $1.1M, and growth bands kick in above $1.1M.

What this means in practice: your $120k base is fixed and never moves with business performance. Your variable comp only flows when the business is doing well enough to cover the cost — when CM is positive past the $1.0M baseline. Above that, the bands shown below kick in. The intent is that you and the business win together.

A note on the $1.1M-$1.4M zone. The rate dips to 5% in this band and recovers to 7.5% above $1.4M. This shape reflects the cost of the higher base salary being paid back through that growth zone. Once the business has cleared $1.4M CM, the rate climbs again to reward stretch performance.

How Your Variable Compensation Works

The thinking behind the plan, in plain English.

We pay you variable compensation because we want to reward exceptional performance. And we pay you when the customer pays — actual cash in the account means actual cash in your pocket.

That single sentence is the spine of the whole plan. Everything below follows from it. These are the principles we use to design how the bands, gates, and bonuses actually work — and the deal we’re making with you when you sign on.

The six principles

  1. Cash in is cash out. The day a customer’s cash lands at MEC is the day it counts toward your commission. Not the day a quote is sent. Not the day a booking is confirmed. The day the cash actually arrives. This is different to the old plan, and it does mean some commission will land later than it used to — especially for events booked months in advance. But it also means the cash you’re paid on is real, and the company isn’t out of pocket if a deal gets pulled.
  2. Same role, same plan. Every Sales Rep is on the same plan as every other Sales Rep. Every Performer is on the same plan as every other Performer. Where current rates differ from the standard shape today, that’s a transition position — we’ll bring everyone onto the same shape at the next plan refresh. Bespoke compensation belongs in your base salary, not in your commission curve.
  3. The harder you push, the more you earn per dollar. Bands climb. The first dollar of GP above your previous tier earns at a higher rate than the last dollar of the tier you just cleared. We want to reward stretch, not punish it. There’s no cap.
  4. Refunds reduce future commission. They never touch your base. If a gig is refunded or cancelled after you’ve been paid commission on it, that refund nets against your next commission cycle. No exceptions. But it never touches your base salary, and your paycheck never goes negative because of a refund.
  5. What lands while you’re employed is yours. If you ever leave MEC, any commission already paid out is yours. Any commission on cash that has already arrived at MEC, even if it hasn’t been paid out yet, is yours — in your final paycheck. Cash that arrives after your last day stays with the company.
  6. KPIs matter where your contract says they do. Some roles have KPIs spelled out in their employment agreement — if yours does, those KPIs are real. They will be tracked, reviewed quarterly, and they affect your eligibility for full variable pay. We will not put a KPI on you that we don’t intend to enforce. If a KPI feels wrong or out of date, raise it.

How "GP Attributed" works (the proportional rule)

When a client pays in instalments — say a deposit now and the balance closer to the event — Zoho splits the booking's total expected Gross Profit across each payment in proportion to the payment's share of the sale. Your commission is calculated only on the GP that has actually landed — never on GP still sitting in unpaid balances.

What we don’t pay variable comp on

Variable compensation is a sharp tool, and we use it for a narrow set of things. Plenty of behaviour we deeply value sits outside it:

  • Helping a colleague close a deal — noticed and recognised, but not split into your commission. That’s the team.
  • Doing your job exceptionally well in non-numerical ways — client experience, hand-offs, mentoring, the way you turn up. Reflected in your annual review and discretionary bonuses, not in the plan multiplier.
  • Effort in itself — we pay for what lands. Effort is necessary but not sufficient.

How the plan changes year to year

Expect a band tweak once a year — a small calibration based on what we’ve learned and where the business is heading. Bigger structural changes (new plan shape, addition of team-pool commissions if we go that way) will be communicated in advance and never applied retrospectively. Cash you’ve already earned is locked in.
