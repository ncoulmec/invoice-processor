Key Terms

Quick reference for the language used on your dashboard.
Revenue (ex GST)
Money the company earns from your performances after stripping the 10% GST. This is what shows in Xero's P&L and what your commission is calculated on.
TTM (Trailing Twelve Months)
Your revenue total over the last 365 days. Updated each quarter — drives your quarterly commission. Smooths out seasonal variation so you don't have to wait until year-end to be paid.
Super-Inclusive
The dollar figures on your dashboard already include the 12% superannuation. So if it says $5,000 commission, that's $4,464 paid into your bank (pre-tax) and $536 paid into your super fund — $5,000 total in your name.
Milestone Bonus
An extra bonus that ramps up linearly between two revenue thresholds — for you, $0 at $70k revenue scaling smoothly to the full $5,000 at $100k. Every dollar of revenue between those two points unlocks a proportional slice. It pays on top of the band-based commission.
Annual True-Up
Year-end catch-up payment. If your final TTM revenue would have earned you more than the sum of your quarterly payouts, the difference is paid as a top-up. See "What is the True-Up?" in the Overview tab for full details.
Quarterly Payout
Your commission split into 4 cash flows. Each quarter, we look at your TTM revenue and pay you 1/4 of the annual commission that level would earn — so cash flow stays steady through the year.
Base Salary
Your guaranteed annual salary, paid fortnightly, regardless of performance. Shown ex-super on your dashboard with super added separately. Increases as you complete project uplifts.
Project Base-Salary Uplift
For your role, completing a project (Rostering System, Quoting Tool, Client Portal) doesn't pay a one-off cash bonus — it permanently raises your base salary by the project amount on Definition of Done sign-off. Hayley/Nathan update Xero on approval day. The Projects tab tickboxes let you model what your future base salary looks like if a project were completed today.
Total Package
Base salary + super on base + commission earned + completed project uplifts. The full amount paid in your name across the year, before the tax man takes his share.
Effective Rate
The commission % you'd earn on the next dollar of revenue at your current position — base band rate × KPI multiplier.
KPI Multiplier
A multiplier applied to your variable comp once KPIs go live, driven by your KPI scorecard (six categories from your employment agreement). Indicative range is roughly 0.7× to 1.3×, but the exact range will be confirmed before activation. Currently locked at 1.0× — KPIs are still in DRAFT.

What is the True-Up?

Your commission is calculated each quarter on your trailing 12-month revenue (TTM) and divided by 4. This means quarterly payouts are smoother and more predictable — you don't have to wait until year-end to get paid.

But there's a problem: if you have a slow start to the year and a big finish, the early quarterly payouts are calculated on lower TTM, so they pay less. By the time your TTM catches up at year-end, only the final quarter's payout reflects your strong year — and that's just 25% of the annual commission. You'd be underpaid.

The Annual True-Up fixes this. At year-end:

  • We compute what your full annual commission should be at your final Q4 TTM revenue.
  • We subtract everything we've already paid you across Q1–Q4.
  • The difference is paid as a year-end catch-up bonus.

This way, if you finish the year strong, you get the full annual commission you earned — not just 1/4 of it from the last quarter. Your base salary is never affected, and the true-up is never negative (no claw-back if you're already overpaid; the next year's commissions absorb that naturally).

Example: Q1 TTM is $130k → quarterly payout $1k. Q4 TTM is $200k → final-quarter payout $3.75k. Total quarterly paid ≈ $9k. But annual entitlement at $200k = $15k. The $6k true-up at year-end gets you to the full $15k.

How Your Variable Compensation Works

The thinking behind your plan, in plain English. Different from the sales team — your plan is built around Performance Revenue, not GP.

Your role brings in revenue in two ways: by performing at gigs MEC books, and by leading the systems work that makes the whole business hum. Your variable comp rewards the first directly. The systems work is rewarded with permanent base salary uplifts on completion — not one-off cash bonuses. Each project you finish raises your base for the rest of your time at MEC.

Below is the spine of the plan and the deal we’re making with you. Performers are paid on a different cycle to the sales team because performance revenue is a quarterly figure, not a monthly one — and your plan is structured to reflect that.

The four principles (performer edition)

  1. Performance Revenue is the metric. Your variable comp is calculated on the total revenue from gigs you personally perform at, plus the gigs you book and manage as a Talent/Systems lead — across the financial year. Not GP, not bookings, not quotes. Revenue you brought through the door — every dollar of cash that arrives from a gig you delivered counts toward your bands.
  2. Paid quarterly, not monthly. Performance Revenue is naturally lumpy — a single big gig can shift a quarter’s number meaningfully. Calculating quarterly (rather than monthly) smooths the noise and lines up with how the business sees its own performance. Your commission lands in the first fortnightly pay cycle of the new quarter, after the previous quarter is closed and reconciled.
  3. Bands climb — the harder you push, the more you earn per dollar. The first dollar of revenue above your previous tier earns at a higher rate than the last dollar of the tier you just cleared. Stretch is rewarded, not punished. There’s no cap.
  4. You need to be employed at quarter-end to be eligible for that quarter’s commission. Different to the sales team, because your variable is a quarterly figure. Anything from previous fully-completed quarters is yours and lands in your final paycheck. Cash that lands after your last day stays with the company. Base salary is never touched.

How Performance Revenue is calculated

We sum every dollar of revenue (ex-GST) that came in from gigs you performed at or led during the financial year. Performances, talent-management revenue, and any retainer fees on engagements you own. The figure flows through your plan’s revenue bands to compute commission, super-inclusive.

Quick example
Q1 performance revenue: $42,000
Q2 performance revenue: $26,000 (slow quarter — kids' school holidays)
Q3 performance revenue: $48,000
Q4 performance revenue: $34,000
Annual total: $150,000 — runs through your plan’s bands to produce commission.

Why this matters: the bands are annualised. A great Q3 doesn’t pay the same as a flat Q1, Q2, and Q3 — because in the end-of-year reconciliation, it’s the annual total that drives which band rates apply. Quarterly payouts are estimates against the annual figure, and the year-end true-up squares them.

Project base-salary uplifts (your second income stream)

On top of revenue commission, you have three projects whose completion permanently raises your base salary — Rostering System, Quoting Tool, Client Portal. Each lifts your base by its stated amount on Definition of Done sign-off, not on time spent or progress. The slider on the Projects tab is a self-tracking tool only — it doesn’t move the trigger.

Why structured this way: project work is genuinely additional, and we want to recognise it permanently rather than as a one-off cash payment. Once a project is signed off, it stays in your base salary for as long as you’re at MEC. Hayley/Nathan update Xero on the day the DOD is approved.

Setting expectations. These three projects are a deliberate, one-off lever to bring your base salary up to the “green” range — your chance to demonstrate you can successfully implement major systems and be permanently rewarded for it. This isn’t a perpetual mechanism. Future major projects are part of the regular Systems Manager role and won’t each automatically add another $2,500 to your base. Some additional project work may feed into a higher base salary down the line, but only via the normal annual salary review process — not as an automatic per-project uplift. Completing project work is an expectation of the role.

The $5,000 milestone bonus (ramp, not cliff)

At $100k in annual performance revenue, you unlock the full $5,000 milestone bonus. To avoid the demoralising "all or nothing at $100k" cliff, the bonus ramps linearly from $70k → $100k. Every dollar of revenue you earn between those two figures unlocks a proportional slice of the bonus.

At $70k revenue → $0 unlocked  ·  at $85k → $2,500 (50%)  ·  at $100k → full $5,000
At $98k (your last 365 actual) → $4,667 (93% unlocked)

What we don’t pay variable comp on

Variable compensation is a sharp tool. Plenty of behaviour we deeply value sits outside it:

  • Doing your job exceptionally well in non-numerical ways — client experience, hand-offs, mentoring, the way you turn up. Reflected in your annual review and discretionary bonuses, not the plan multiplier.
  • Effort in itself — we pay for what lands. Effort is necessary but not sufficient.
  • Iterations, edits and maintenance on systems that already exist — tweaks and improvements to a system you’ve already built and signed off, bug fixes, ongoing software/iOS-style update work that’s required to keep things running. These are part of your normal Systems Manager role, not new project uplifts.
  • Additional projects beyond the three major ones listed on your Projects tab. The three named projects (Rostering System, Quoting Tool, Client Portal) are the only ones that trigger a permanent base-salary uplift. Other project work that sits outside those three is part of your job description and recognised through the annual salary review process — not through additional automatic uplifts.

How the plan changes year to year

Expect a band tweak once a year — a small calibration based on what we’ve learned and where the business is heading. Bigger structural changes (new plan shape, additions like team-pool kickers if we go that way) will be communicated in advance and never applied retrospectively. Cash you’ve already earned is locked in.
